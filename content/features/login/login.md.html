--- title: "Login Screen" date: 2025-08-02T15:56:41-04:00 description: "Login screens, also known as lock screens, provide a means of authentication on a computer or any other device, as well as a way of selecting your user and switching between users. Some operating systems have had the ability to log in and support multiple users since the 70s and 80s, but nearly all were mainframe or workstation operating systems, and were also command-line. The earliest graphical login screens really start to show up in the mid 90s, when more consumer-centric operating systems began including multi-user functionality, as well as providing a means of protecting said accounts with a password. These early graphical login screens were typically simple dialog boxes. Additionally, they were often rather insecure, with some operating systems like Windows 9x not only not requiring a password, but also actually allowing you to get into the computer just by pressing the Cancel button. By the early 00s however, login screens saw an overhaul, with full screen login screens, and you could just click on your user account, rather than having to type it out. This also allowed you to have a unique profile picture. Additionally, security was prioritized much more, now requiring a password, and having differentation between administrator, standard, and guest accounts. Moreover, some operating systems began introducing Fast User Switching, allowing a user to quickly sign out without exiting their session, and allow someone else to use the computer, such as Windows XP and Mac OS X Panther. By the early 10s, login screens changed dramatically once again, taking greater influence from mobile operating systems. In the process, the terminology changed from login screen to lock screen, you could swipe or use gestures to then sign in, new methods of logging in were added, such as patterns, facial recognition, PINs, and fingerprint scanning, and way more information was added to login screens, such as the ability to display notifications on the lock screen, and eventually, the displaying of ads, for better or worse. Additionally, other devices other than PCs would get login screens over time as they got more complicated and PC-like, particularly by the late 00s, such as game consoles and smartphones. In the case of smartphones specifically, the more individualistic and personal nature of them means they typically don't prioritize multi-user logins, instead simply serving as a method of authentication." ---
