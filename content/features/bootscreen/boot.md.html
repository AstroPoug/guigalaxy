--- title: "Boot Screen" date: 2025-07-28T18:38:27-04:00 description: "Ever since PCs have existed, they have needed to boot. However, early on, booting typically tended to be text-based, like everything else, and it was often verbose, telling you exactly what it was doing as it booted. However, with the rise of GUI-based operating systems in the mid 80s, a more graphical means of booting the computer became necessary, as to showcase how user friendly these new operating systems were right from the start. Thus, the first true boot screens as we know them emerge during this era. From the mid 80s to early 90s, most of them were static, due to limited graphical capabilities, with a few exceptions such as Windows 1.0-2.0. By the mid 90s, however, more advanced computers were able to display more advanced and often animated boot screens. Since then, they've been a staple of operating systems. That said, different operating systems have different approaches to their boot screens. For example, Windows typically has a throbber alongside the version logo, whilst Mac OS typically has an actual progress bar, often with somewhat verbose information about what the OS is doing as it boots. iOS goes in a more minimalistic direction, simply showing the Apple logo, and nothing else. Over time, as devices became more advanced, they would gain boot screens as well, such as game consoles in the mid 90s, and cellphones in the mid 00s." ---
